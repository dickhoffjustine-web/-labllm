Tab 01 — Literature Review & Writing

Use AI to map the field,
not to think for you.

AI is genuinely useful for getting an overview, spotting patterns, and drafting structure. The intellectual argument stays yours. Here's how to use it well.

The golden rule
Always verify every claim, citation, and factual statement. LLMs hallucinate confidently. Use AI to scaffold your review, then fill it with real sources you've actually read.
Step 1 🗺️
Map the landscape
Ask the model to explain the major schools of thought, key debates, and foundational papers in your field. Use this as a starting map, not a finished picture.
Step 2 🔍
Find your gaps
Describe your research question and ask what's been studied, what's contested, and what's genuinely open. Good for generating search terms and framing your contribution.
Step 3 ✍️
Draft & restructure
Paste in your bullet-point notes and ask the model to draft a coherent narrative. Then rewrite in your own voice. This is "AI as a scaffold," not ghostwriting.
Watch out ⚠️
Hallucinated citations
Never trust a citation from an LLM without checking it. Fake papers, wrong authors, wrong years — it happens all the time. Use Semantic Scholar, Google Scholar, or Zotero to verify.
Prompt templates you can steal
Map a field
I'm a PhD student in [field]. Give me a structured overview of the main theoretical traditions, key debates, and 5–8 foundational papers in [specific topic]. For each tradition, note what it explains well and where it falls short.
Identify gaps
My research question is: [your question]. Based on what is typically covered in the literature on [topic], what are the most commonly cited gaps? What methodological approaches have been underused?
Synthesise your notes
Here are my notes on 5 papers on [topic]: [paste notes] Please synthesise these into a coherent 3-paragraph narrative that shows how the arguments relate and build on each other. Flag any tensions or contradictions you notice.
Improve your own draft
Here is a draft paragraph from my literature review: [paste paragraph] Please give me specific feedback on: (1) logical flow, (2) whether the argument is clearly structured, (3) any claims that seem unsupported. Do not rewrite it — just give feedback.
Tools to combine with AI
Semantic Scholar and Connected Papers are excellent for finding real literature. Use AI to understand and synthesise; use these tools to find and verify. Zotero with the browser extension is the standard for managing sources.
Tab 02 — Ethics & Privacy

The stuff your supervisor
will actually ask about.

Using AI in research isn't plug-and-play. There are real questions about data privacy, authorship, transparency, and research integrity. This tab won't lecture you — it'll just flag what matters.

🟢 Generally fine to do
  • Use AI to draft or restructure your own text
  • Run publicly available datasets through AI tools
  • Use AI for literature mapping and search
  • Ask AI to explain methods or concepts to you
  • Use AI for code generation (cite it like a tool)
  • Run local/offline models on sensitive data
🔴 Proceed with caution
  • Uploading participant data to cloud AI tools
  • Submitting AI-generated text without disclosure
  • Using AI for qualitative coding without checking
  • Trusting AI analysis of sensitive documents
  • Using proprietary data in external LLM APIs
  • Listing AI as a co-author (most journals reject this)
Privacy rule of thumb
If it's participant data, medical data, or anything covered by GDPR — do not upload it to ChatGPT, Claude.ai, or any cloud API without explicit legal clearance. Use a local model (Ollama + LLaMA 3, Mistral) instead. Your ethics approval probably doesn't cover third-party cloud processing.
Local LLMs for sensitive data
Free & local 🦙
Ollama + LLaMA 3
Runs entirely on your laptop. No data leaves your machine. Install Ollama, pull llama3, run it from the terminal. Good enough for most research tasks.
Local GUI 🖥️
LM Studio
A desktop app for running local models with a ChatGPT-like interface. No coding needed. Works on Mac, Windows, Linux. Good for qualitative analysis of sensitive docs.
For coders 🐍
Hugging Face Transformers
Full programmatic control. Run models locally in Python. Steep learning curve but gives you the most flexibility for research pipelines.
Authorship & transparency
EU Guidelines say
The EU ERA Guidelines on Responsible Use of Generative AI in Research (2024) recommend: disclose which AI tools were used and how, do not list AI as an author, take full responsibility for AI-assisted content, and do not use AI to peer review confidential manuscripts.
Disclosure language templates
Methods section disclosure
Parts of this manuscript were drafted with the assistance of [model name, e.g. Claude 3.5 Sonnet], a large language model. All AI-generated content was reviewed, edited, and verified by the authors. The AI tool was not involved in data collection, analysis, or the intellectual framing of the research.
Ask AI to help you write the disclosure itself
I used AI assistance in the following ways during this research: [list]. Help me write a concise, honest disclosure statement suitable for a methods section in a [field] journal. It should be transparent without overstating the AI's role.
Tab 03 — Data, Visualization & Statistics

From messy data
to publication-ready figures.

AI is genuinely great at writing analysis code, explaining statistical output, and helping you choose the right visualisation. Here's how to use it without outsourcing your thinking.

Recommended tools
Python · pandas Python · matplotlib Python · seaborn Python · plotly R · ggplot2 R · tidyr R · statsmodels Datawrapper Observable / D3.js JASP (GUI stats)
Python 📦
Generate analysis code
Describe your dataset and what you want to do. Ask for working pandas/matplotlib or ggplot2 code. Always read and understand the code before running it — don't run blindly.
Stats 📐
Explain statistical output
Paste your R or Python output and ask what it means. Great for interpreting p-values, confidence intervals, model diagnostics, or regression tables if you're unsure.
Viz 🎨
Choose the right chart
Describe your data (e.g. "two groups, continuous outcome, non-normal") and ask which chart type fits best and why. Also useful for accessibility and colour-blind-safe palettes.
Caution 🧮
Don't outsource interpretation
AI can describe what statistical output says. It cannot tell you what it means for your research question. That interpretation is yours — that's the actual science.
Prompt templates — data & viz
Generate Python visualisation code
I have a pandas DataFrame called `df` with columns: [list columns and types]. I want to create a [chart type] showing [what relationship]. Use matplotlib/seaborn, make it publication-ready (clean style, axis labels, no chartjunk), and use a colourblind-safe palette.
Generate R / ggplot2 code
I have an R dataframe called `df` with variables: [list]. I want a ggplot2 figure showing [what]. Use theme_minimal(), add axis labels and a title, and make it suitable for a journal submission (300dpi when exported).
Explain statistical output
Here is the output from a [test name, e.g. mixed ANOVA] in [R/Python/SPSS]: [paste output] Please explain: (1) what each value means, (2) how to report this in APA format, and (3) any assumptions I should check given this output.
Debug your code
This Python/R code gives me the following error: [paste error message] Here is the code: [paste code] What's wrong, and what's the fix? Explain why it happened.

Tab 04 — Methodology & Research Design

AI for which kind
of research?

Not all AI tools fit all research. This tab helps you figure out where AI genuinely adds value across qualitative, quantitative, and mixed-methods work — and where you should think twice.

Method / Task AI fit How to use it Watch out for
Systematic literature review High Screening abstracts, drafting PRISMA summaries, organising themes Hallucinated papers; always verify with real databases
Qualitative coding (thematic analysis) Medium Generating initial codes, checking inter-rater consistency, finding patterns in transcripts AI codes surface patterns; you interpret meaning. Never skip human review.
Quantitative data analysis High Writing analysis code, explaining output, choosing tests, debugging AI can misread your data structure; always inspect code before running
Survey design High Drafting items, checking for leading questions, translating scales, writing introductory text AI doesn't know your population; pilot test anyway
Interviews & ethnography Low–Medium Transcription cleanup, interview guide drafting, memo writing AI cannot replace fieldwork judgment; use with caution on sensitive material
Experimental design Medium Power analysis code, counterbalancing schemes, protocol drafting Always consult a statistician for novel designs; AI can get power calculations wrong
Computational / NLP research High Scripting pipelines, model selection, interpreting evaluation metrics Keeping up with latest methods — AI training data has a cutoff
Clinical / biomedical research Low Background reading, protocol drafting, writing assistance Never use cloud AI for patient data. Regulatory compliance is your responsibility.
Historical / archival research Medium OCR cleanup, translation assistance, contextualising sources AI may not know niche historical contexts; factual errors likely
Useful prompts by stage
Research design feedback
Here is my research design: - Research question: [question] - Method: [method] - Data: [what you're collecting] - Analysis plan: [brief description] Please give me critical feedback on: (1) internal consistency, (2) potential validity threats, (3) ethical considerations I may have missed.
Choose the right statistical test
I have the following data: [describe variables, types, distribution if known]. My research question is [question]. I want to know [relationship/difference/prediction]. What statistical test is most appropriate and why? What are the assumptions I need to check?
Qualitative coding assistant
I am doing thematic analysis using [approach, e.g. Braun & Clarke]. Here is a short extract from an interview transcript: [paste excerpt] Please suggest 3–5 initial codes that capture what is happening in this excerpt. Do not interpret — just label. I will do the interpretation myself.
Tab 05 — Grant Writing

The tab that doesn't exist
in any other course.

Grant writing is almost entirely absent from AI-for-researchers curricula. That's a missed opportunity. AI is genuinely useful here — for structure, language, and anticipating reviewer objections.

Where AI actually helps
AI won't write a winning grant for you — reviewers notice generic prose. But it's great for drafting the impact statement, sharpening the significance section, writing the lay summary, and stress-testing your logic by playing devil's advocate.
High value 📝
Lay summaries
Turn your technical abstract into plain language for non-specialist panels or public outreach sections. Huge time saver, and AI is genuinely good at this.
High value 🎯
Significance & impact
Describe your project and ask AI to draft an impact section. Use it as a starting point, then add specific context, partners, and outcomes that only you know.
Medium value 🔁
Devil's advocate
Paste your research plan and ask "what would a sceptical reviewer object to?" This surfaces weak spots before the panel does. Genuinely useful.
Medium value 📋
Work plan & timeline
Give AI your project phases and ask it to generate a Gantt-style work plan breakdown or draft the work package descriptions. Edit heavily.
Grant prompt templates
Write a lay summary
Here is the abstract of my research project: [paste abstract] Please rewrite this as a 150-word lay summary for a non-specialist audience (e.g. a research council panel including non-experts). It should be engaging, jargon-free, and clearly explain why this research matters to society.
Strengthen impact section
Here is my current impact/significance section: [paste text] My funder is [funder name, e.g. NWO / ERC / Horizon Europe]. Please suggest specific ways to strengthen this section in terms of: (1) clarity of contribution, (2) societal relevance, (3) how well it speaks to this funder's stated priorities.
Anticipate reviewer objections
Here is my research proposal: [paste proposal or key sections] Please act as a sceptical but fair peer reviewer. List the 5 most likely objections a reviewer would raise — about feasibility, novelty, methodology, or impact. For each objection, suggest how I could pre-emptively address it in the proposal.
Draft a work plan
My project has the following phases: [list phases and rough durations] The project runs for [X months/years]. Please draft a work plan description with work packages (WP1, WP2...), each with objectives, activities, deliverables, and responsible team members. I'll fill in the specific names and deliverables.
Tab 06 — Prompting Playground

Learn by doing.
Break things. Try stuff.

This is your sandbox. Write any prompt, load one of the exercises below, and see what Claude does with it. The best way to get good at prompting is to experiment — not read about it.

What makes a good research prompt?
Role — tell it who it is ("Act as a methods reviewer...")
Context — give it your situation ("I'm a PhD student in cognitive psychology...")
Task — be specific about what you want ("give me 5 alternative framings...")
Format — say how you want the answer ("bullet points, max 150 words")
Constraint — limit what it does ("do not rewrite my text, only give feedback")

Prompting concepts — quick reference
Zero-shot prompting
Give the task directly, no examples. Works well for simple, well-defined tasks. "Summarise this abstract in 2 sentences."
Simple tasks
Few-shot prompting
Give 2–3 examples of what you want before the actual task. Dramatically improves consistency for formatting and tone.
Formatting · Coding
Chain of thought
Ask the model to "think step by step" before answering. Improves performance on complex reasoning and multi-step problems.
Complex reasoning
Role prompting
Assign an identity: "You are a methods reviewer for Nature." Shifts the model's framing and often improves domain-specific responses.
Domain expertise
Self-critique loop
After getting a response, prompt: "Now critique what you just wrote. What's weak or missing?" Then ask for a revised version.
Quality improvement
Constraint prompting
Explicitly tell it what NOT to do. "Do not rewrite my text. Only identify logical gaps." Stops AI from overstepping.
Feedback tasks
Copy-and-modify prompt starters
Role + task + constraint
You are a senior researcher in [field]. I am going to give you a paragraph from my introduction. Your task is to give me feedback on the logical structure only — do not rewrite it, do not comment on grammar. Just tell me where the argument is unclear or unsupported. Paragraph: [paste here]
Chain of thought for method choice
Think step by step. My research question is: [question]. My data is: [describe]. Step 1: What are the three most appropriate statistical tests for this situation? Step 2: For each, what are the key assumptions? Step 3: Which do you recommend and why?
